About

I am a Lecturer in Sociology at Queen's University Belfast, where I have been based since September 2014. My research centres on the relationship between emotions and power, developing a process-relational theoretical framework that connects micro-level interaction, meso-level institutional dynamics, and macro-level political processes.

My primary research interests are interdisciplinary, lying at the intersections of sociology and politics. Emotion and power, along with affect and habitus, remain central to my ongoing research agenda in social theory and within the political sociology of emotions, as well as to my wider interests in the sociology and politics of Ireland, the digital sociology of emotion, and qualitative research methods.

I am currently completing a monograph for Routledge, provisionally titled Emotions & Power: Habitus and Change in Late Modernity. I am a Fellow of the The Institute for Global Peace, Security and Justice and an Associate Fellow of the Institute of Irish Studies at QUB.

I completed my PhD in Sociology & Politics at the National University of Ireland, Galway in 2014, funded by an Irish Research Council 'Government of Ireland Scholarship'. Prior to that, I returned to education as a mature student in 2004 and was awarded a BA (Hons) in Sociology & Politics, and Economics, at NUI Galway in 2007, graduating first in my class, with a double first, and receiving a departmental fellowship for postgraduate study.

Research

Emotions & Power: A Process-Relational Framework

My central research programme develops a multi-scalar theoretical framework for understanding the relationship between emotions and power. Drawing on process philosophy, relational sociology, and the sociology of emotions, this work connects micro-level interaction with meso-level institutional dynamics and macro-level political processes. Key concepts include emotional capital, processual approaches to the habitus, and the interplay of emotions and power within Bourdieu's 'field of power'. This programme underpins my forthcoming Routledge monograph.

Monograph · Ongoing

The Emotional State

This project examines how the nation-state has been involved in the construction and deconstruction of the emotional life of the polity, how it reflects dominant emotional regimes, and how these processes change through time. It critically re-considers classical, modern, and contemporary state theory from a political sociology of emotions perspective. Supported by a BA/Leverhulme Small Research Grant (2015–2016).

Funded Research · 2016–present

Emotional Swindling in Populist Politics

A current project examining how trust, affect, and the performance of authenticity operate within populist political discourse. Investigates the concept of 'emotional swindling' and its implications for democratic politics.

Paper in Progress · 2025–

Embodied Nationalism

Building on my 2013 chapter on emotions and nationalism, this project investigates the emotional and embodied dimensions of national identification through the concept of 'national habitus'. The 2013 chapter has been widely cited and was translated into Croatian for a 2025 edited collection.

Ongoing

ÆRN — The Affect and Emotions Research Network

Co-organised with Dr Clara Fischer, ÆRN brings together scholars working on affect and emotions across the social sciences and humanities. The network convenes workshops, supports collaborative research, and fosters interdisciplinary dialogue.

Research Network · Ongoing

EMOPOLIS — Emotional Power and the Body Politic

A major research programme currently in development that places emotions, bodies, and power at the centre of the scientific investigation of politics and the state. EMOPOLIS advances the concept of the 'emotional state' — examining how modern states construct, regulate, and reproduce the emotional life of the polity — alongside a novel framework for understanding the conversion of emotional capital into political capital. The project combines theoretical innovation with a pioneering 'carnal political ethnography' of embodied emotional practice, with Ireland and the UK as comparative cases. A competitive funding bid is currently in preparation.

Teaching

My primary teaching interests are in classical and contemporary social theory, political sociology, the sociology of emotions, and the sociology and politics of Ireland. I teach the core social theory modules at both undergraduate and postgraduate levels and have developed specialist modules on emotions and politics.
